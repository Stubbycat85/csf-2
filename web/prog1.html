Or: Introduction to Programming and Computation using Python

The Evergreen State College

This is the first of a three-quarter sequence of classes that will teach you basic programming skills motivated by processing large datasets in real-life, including political elections and DNA sequences. It is taught in the Python programming language. It combines the threads for a Problem-Solving Workshop and Introductory Programming from the Computer Science Foundations program.

Manifesto

Programming is a tool for expressing your values in a creative, useful way using computers. Like other human artifacts, programs can be interesting in themselves in addition to performing useful tasks. In this thread you will learn how empowering it is to think with computers and how to use them to solve problems using information from everyday life. These datasets could come from political elections, scientific experiments, popular websites, or even your own life. To solve these problems, you will incidentally learn the basics of a programming language called Python, how to work with software tools and files, debugging skills, graphical plotting and data visualization, and social coding practices.

Topics

  • Python script files and interpreter
  • Basic file input/output operations
  • Introspective tools, like UNIX man and Python dir
  • Python syntax, expressions, values, variables, constants, literals, types, assignment
  • Classes, objects, data members
  • Functions, parameters and arguments
  • Lists, tuples, dictionaries
  • Iteration, list comprehensions
  • Debugging
  • Plotting graphs, visualizing data
  • Making sense of a dataset from real life.
  • Using Git, Github, and social coding practices.

Meeting Times and Activities

Mondays 1pm-3pm, Sem II A1107

Puzzle-Solving, Academic Statement Work, and Open-Ended Discussion aka Workshop and Seminar.

This period consists of a mix of three main activities: solving puzzles, writing your academic statement, and discussing programming in seminar.

The first activity develops intuition and skills in math, logic, and symbolic reasoning. This is to solve mathematical, logical, or symbolic games and riddles puzzles, riddles, and games that are mathematical, logical, or symbolic in nature. This will aid you in your work in the Discrete Math thread, as well as make you a better programmer. You'll be able to detect bad arguments in real-life, make good arguments (and win!), enlighten your friends, and confound your enemies.

This will alternate with the second activity, six hours of work on your Academic Statement, which is a new graduation requirement.

The third activity, which will be interspersed with the first two, is an open-ended seminar discussion in small groups. The discussion will motivate the practice of programming by beginning with a chosen topic and allowing students to generate questions and guide their own learning. The topic for the coming week (listed above) will be motivated by first presenting a short problem statement, possibly the homework assignment for that week, and then working backwards to construct the necessary concepts. Be prepared for awkward silences, animated discussion, intense thought, confusion, and understanding.

Tuesdays 10am-12noon, LH 05

Inspiration and Interaction, Live Coding on stage, and Guest Speakers, (aka Lecture)

Thursdays 10am-12noon, 1pm - 3pm, 3pm - 5pm Lib 2610 (ACC)

Design Critique and Programming Practice (aka Lab)

These are smaller class meetings than the lecture and more interactive. We will begin with a design critique, where students will post a printout of their code (their solutions to the homework assigned the previous week) on a wall and practice giving and receiving feedback to each other under my guidance. This feedback is for programming style, and not necessarily correctness. This practice is inspired by critiques in art and design classes.

Next, students will grade each others programming homework for correctness by running their submissions on computers in the lab.

After that, students will take an individual written quiz on paper to demonstrate their knowledge of coding gained that week. This is the only exercise in class where collaboration is not allowed. This is a short program that they will grade in-class by typing it into a Python interpreter and providing feedback to each other.

Finally, I will introduce the next programming homework for the following week, and students can begin working on it.

Student only need attend one of the following lab sessions according to this schedule.

Collaboration

You are allowed to discuss and share code at all times, including using code from the internet, except during individual written exercises on Thursdays. Please name all your contributors or sources in the comments of your submitted code, to give them proper credit, and be prepared to understand or discuss any line of your code during design critique. If you have any questions at any time about what is acceptable collaboration, please talk to me or the TA.

Assignments

There are four types of assignments, each due weekly:

  1. math puzzles, due in-class on Monday
  2. a journal entry, due on Moodle on Friday at 5pm
  3. individual written quiz, due in-class on Thursday
  4. programming homework, due using csf_submit on Wednesday at 11:59pm
All assignments will be graded and will prompt feedback from students to their peers, under my guidance. I will personally grade a statistical sampling of all student work, but all students are expected to complete all assignments. I will also do all of these assignments along with the students, either to provide a model before they hand in their assignment, or as an answer key after they submit their solutions.

The due days and times given will be strictly observed for counting work as on-time. Late assignments will be accepted for credit, but they will be noted as late in your end-of-quarter evaluation.

The math puzzles are assigned in the Monday workshops and are meant to be completed within an hour while working in a group. Any individual must be able to explain their solution to another group. Paper handouts will be given in workshop. Attendance will be taken by submitting paper submissions by the end of class, at which time I will also provide solutions on paper.

The journal entries are due on Moodle by Friday at 5pm. More specific instructions will be posted here soon. They are considered a form of creative expression and a test of technical writing skill. They can be up to three paragraphs in length and should address the following items over the course of the past week:

  • What specific concepts and ideas you learned
  • What new questions were raised that you are curious about and want to learn more about in the coming weeks
  • How the items above will help you achieve your programming goals
  • Connections to previous journal entries.
I will write my own journal entry, documenting my own experience in preparing and executing the class, and present it to the class as a model in lecture on Tuesday.

The individual written quiz are given in Thursday labs after grading the programming homework for the past week. Students will solve a short programming problem (up to 4 lines of code) on paper meant to exercise a concept taught in the past week, without the aid of interactive computer feedback. Quizzes will be graded in-class by students, and then returned the following week.

The programming homework is the longest and most involved of the assignments. It requires writing a Python program of up to 100 lines of code to solve a relatively open-ended problem related to data processing. It is due using the csf_submit program on Wednesday night at 11:59pm, otherwise it will be counted as late.

Credit and Attendance

Credit will be granted on an all-or-nothing basis for the Problem-Solving Workshop thread and the Introductory Programming thread. You must complete all (one hundred percent) of the work listed in the Assignments section above by the end of the quarter in order to receive credit. Assignments can be submitted late, but they will be noted as such in end-of-quarter evaluations.

Attendance will be taken at every class meeting. If a student misses too many consecutive meetings, we will try to contact you. Missing attendance or late arrivals will not affect your credit, but it will be noted in your evaluations.

I intend to help every student pass this class who is willing to put in the extra work. Your current progress towards getting credit is reflected in your Moodle grade report. If you have any questions at any time about your progress in this class, please feel free to contact me or the TA.

Limiting Distractions

During the lecture period (Tuesday mornings) no personal electronic devices will be allowed (no personal laptops, no phones, no tablets, no MP3 players). All notes should be taken on paper, so stock up on analog writing tools like pens and pencils. Ironic, I know, for a class presumably about computers.

All interactive programming exercises during this time will be done on provided ChromeBooks shared in groups of three. You are asked not to use the ChromeBooks for personal reasons (Gmail, Facebook, Twitter, Skype, Reddit, etc.) and not to sign into your personal Google account on the ChromeBooks (use the guest account only). I ask you and your group partners to help maintain each other's attention on the lecture.

Optional Activities

There are two optional activities throughout the year.

The first activity is reading the recommended book for the class, Lauren Ipsum by Carlos Ytalo-Buena (fact-check). This is a whimsical book about computer science which uses no computers in the style of Alice in Wonderland. It is purely meant for enjoyment and to prompt deeper questions and discussions in class. It is sold out at the Greener Store, but you can buy it online.

The second activity is dinner. Throughout the year, I'll invite you in groups of four or five to my home for potluck dinners. Each of you will receive exactly one surprise personal paper invitation at the postal address you provide on the first day of class, on some unannounced date. Attendance is completely optional and will not affect your credit or evaluation in any way, but I do ask for prompt RSVP (which I will accept electronically).

This will be an opportunity for philosophical discussions and candid questions and answers about anything and everything: the CSF program, Lauren Ipsum, computer science in general, jobs, life, music for robots, Evergreen, you name it. Dinner will be preceded by a brief work session where you are asked to help me do planning and provide feedback for the coming week.
