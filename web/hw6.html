Due: Friday, November 15th at 5:00 PM, as much as you can do in 7 hours total (working in a group, reading, looking up on the internet, coding, talking to tutors, grading, but NOT counting class time). This approach is known as timeboxing and requires you to develop some self-discipline and self-knowledge. At first, it may seem unusual or uncomfortable for you to turn in work that you don't consider complete. However, in real-life, you will not always be able to complete your work perfectly. Everyone's abilities are at a different level, and it is more important for you to understand your own abilities at the present moment and take action (asking for help, or redefining the problem) than to get the right answer.
Submit your work via GitHub, with the tag hw6.
Files to turn in: hw6-ours.html, hw6-theirs.html, hw6.py. .

In this assignment, you will practice meta-learning, that is, learning about learning. You will do this to improve the text of Homework 5 to make it clearer as well as improve your own understanding and ability to complete Homework 5 in the future. Homework 5 presented a steep learning curve. By doing this homework, you will be coming up with new homework problems to help make this learning curve more gentle for yourselves and for future students.

You are working in a group with two or three members to come up with five sample problems that will lead someone to the solution of Homework 5, Problem 1, which you will write in the file hw6-ours.html. Your group will exchange problems with another group, renaming the file you receive as hw6-theirs.html. You will work on the problems that the other group wrote in the file hw6.py. You will commit all three of these files to your GitHub repo, then give the other group your repos for them to grade your work. These steps are described in more detail in Problems 3-8 below. You will be creating new files hw6-ours.html, hw6-theirs.html and hw6.py in your repo. As a team, you will all work on the same files and commit the same files to your individual GitHub repositories, tagging them as hw6.

Your group was chosen to have different levels of ability and experience. For those of you who were able to make more progress on Homework 5, teaching it to your teammates will help you retain the information better. Studies show that explaining information to yourself or others helps you remember more information that you've learned. You will be providing guidance to your group in identifying key concepts that are needed by your teammates to solve Homework 5, Problem 1. For those of you who were not able to finish the first problem of Homework 5, getting individual help from a classmate can give quicker feedback and an alternative explanation, in case the instructor's explanations are not clear or delayed in time. Your job will be to help design programs together to demonstrate the key concepts that you identify. So far you have been reading problem descriptions and code written by the course staff. Now it is your turn to write (simpler) versions of problem descriptions.

All members of your group will receive the same credit for this assignment. If all of you complete it as described below, you will all get credit. If one of you does not complete all the problems, none of you will get credit. Please help your teammates and pay attention to each other's work.

Identify five key concepts necessary to solve Homework 5, Problem 1, then write a short Python coding problem that requires the answerer to demonstrate understanding of this concept. This is the text that you will put in your file hw6-ours.html under the sections called Problem 3, Problem 4, Problem 5, Problem 6, and Problem 7.

You may need to identify one concept and write a problem for it first, before being able to see that another concept is itself necessary to understand it. For an example, see hw6-ex.html, where we have provided some sample problems. Let's say you identify one concept, which is "Creating Nested Data Structures". So you write Problem 4 with the title "Creating Nested Data Structures." But in writing that problem, you realize you need another concept, which is how to create normal, non-nested data structures. So you name Problem 5 "Creating non-nested Data Structures" and write a problem for that as well.

You should not use the example problems in hw6-ex.html verbatim. But you may change them to create similar problems, if they describe a key concept that is blocking your teammates from understanding Homework 5 Problem 1. You should spend about 3-4 hours on writing these problems.
